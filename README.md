# Taiwan Diet Localization Guide（台灣飲食本地化指南）

A practical, research-backed guide to applying evidence-based dietary patterns — Mediterranean, DASH, low-GI, and 211 plate — to Taiwan's everyday food environment: convenience stores, self-serve buffets, night markets, and home cooking.

> Maintained by the team at [metabolab.tw](https://metabolab.tw) — Taiwan-based evidence-based nutrition research.

---

## Why This Exists

Most dietary guidelines and meal plans are written for Western food environments. A Taiwanese person trying to follow the Mediterranean diet or DASH protocol faces a gap: the official recommendations say "whole grains, olive oil, legumes" — but their daily reality is rice boxes, 7-11 shelves, and beef noodle soup.

This repository provides:
- **Food swap tables** — direct substitutions that preserve nutritional intent
- **Ordering guides** — how to apply each diet pattern at Taiwan's most common meal venues
- **Ingredient reference** — Taiwan-available ingredients that match Western equivalents
- **Quantified examples** — portion sizes in familiar Taiwan units (碗、份、盒)

## Scientific Basis

All dietary frameworks referenced here are based on published research:

- **Mediterranean Diet**: Estruch et al. (2013, 2018), *PREDIMED* study — *NEJM*
- **DASH Diet**: Appel et al. (1997), *DASH* trial — *NEJM*; Taiwan MOH DASH adaptation guidelines
- **Low-GI**: Jenkins et al. foundational GI research; Taiwan TFND glycemic index data
- **211 Plate**: Harvard Healthy Eating Plate (2011); Taiwan Health Promotion Administration (HPA) adaptation

Taiwan-specific food composition data sourced from:
> 衛生福利部食品藥物管理署 — 台灣食品營養成分資料庫（TFND）  
> https://consumer.fda.gov.tw/Food/TFND.aspx

## Scope and Limitations

- All sodium/calorie values are approximations based on available published data
- Restaurant and chain store items vary by outlet; use as reference, not exact measurement
- This guide is for educational purposes only and does not constitute medical or nutritional advice
- Individuals with specific health conditions (diabetes, hypertension, kidney disease) should consult a registered dietitian
